## Route

The Shimanami Kaido is a 60km long toll road that connects Japan's main island of Honshu to the island of Shikoku, passing over six small islands in the Seto Inland Sea.

Basically I rode a bike from Onomichi to Imabari. Onomichi is on the south of the mainland in Horishima prefecture (not in Hiroshima the city). Imabari is a town in the North of the island of Shikoku, south of the mainland.

If you're confused then just look at a map.

There's a cycle route of the Shiminami Kaido that is 76km. That's what I'm doing.

## Setting sail. 76km to go

The trip started in the Onomichi cycle terminal, where I picked up a bike I'd rented. Renting isn't necessary but it's better as you guarantee a bike is available (really only a problem in peak season), and you get a shorter queue to pick up said bike.

After being outfitted with a green city bike, I followed the blue road to the ferry. I accidentally went the wrong way for about a hundred metres then turned around and headed to the ferry. It was very close to the bike terminal, close enough that they tell you to just walk there with your bike.

The ferry was ¥110, about 50p, and departs every 10 minutes. It's about 5 minutes to get to the first island.

My bike had 7 gears.

Then I started cycling, followed a group of experienced riders for about as long as I could keep up with them for. After a bit, they rode out of distance, until they stopped and allowed me to overtake for all of about 30 seconds, before again riding out of distance. This would be a general theme of the early stages.

## Early stages

The first 15km went by fairly swiftly, as one might expect with fresh legs and blissful ignorance. After that it was a bit of a struggle. The views were incredible though. I lucked out with the weather, blue skies, cool temperatures, not too much wind. Cycling along the shores. Couldn't ask for better conditions.

One of the big milestone in this ride is when you reach a bridge. You are given a gentle, but still challenging, climb up to each bridge, then ride across and back down to the path on the other side. The first climb was tough but fun, and gave a feeling of satisfaction at the top. Also nice views.

One of the best things (or worst, depending on your standpoint) about the Shimanami Kaido is that you basically just follow a blue line the whole way, so you don't need to sort out any navigation. The blue line also tells you how far you are from the finish line, in kilometres. I'm usually a miles person but you work with what you've got.

## 50km to go

Between 50-40km remaining I struggled a lot. Partly with the knowledge of how far I had left, and because that's when my legs started to get tired. I didn't really have a strategy in terms of pacing, but I figured if I just keep pedalling then eventually I'd get there.

When I hired my bike, the person at the terminal said it would take around 7 hours. The map she gave me said 8-10. My pessimistic brain said that it would probably be closer to 10, given I'm an unexperienced cyclist, with the slowest bike they have and not a very strong athlete in general. Also carrying a slightly too heavy bag.

Part of my struggle at this point in the trip was panicking about whether I'd even get there in time. I started at 8:45, and 10 hours would take me to 6:45pm. The terminal in Imabari closes at 7.

It made me reluctant to take longer breaks from cycling, which would have allowed my legs to recover some strength and freshness, and so ride faster and get up to a gear where I'm cruising along. As it was, I took shorter breaks on weary legs, and trundled along in between making barely any progress.

## 40km to go

This continued until I finished my climb of the second bridge. About 40km to go. I was completely gassed so had a longer break, including starting my sanwich I'd packed for lunch. With some extra energy, I decided to up the pace along the flat bridge. As I went through the gears, I realised that once you're up to speed, 7th gear is pretty much the same effort as when I was trundling along in 3rd, but you're just covering *so much* more ground. I crossed the halfway point on this bridge, which gave it's own energy boost. But mainly I'd figured out *how* to cycle these long distances.

## 30km to go

Given how quickly I'd knocked out these next 10km, I was also not worried about making it back in time. I reasoned that even crawling to the finish line (forshadowing?) I'd still get there with plenty of time, which was a relief.

Obviously riding much faster, I was getting tired more quickly, if measuring by time. If measuring by distane though it wasn't close, I was covering way more ground between breaks. Mentally as well it was reassuring to see the distance remaining ticking down faster.

## 20km to go

I was really properly tired. My legs were aching to the point that I was struggling to get off my bike every time I took a break, and the ache didn't really go away, just got less bad with breaks.

The climbs to bridges were now daunting, and I had to take breaks at the bottom and top of the climbs. They were also slow, I could barely manage to get out of first gear.

Around this time as well, I had a bit of a moment where I nearly joined what I think was a motorway, only to be shoted to stop by a toll guard thingy person. He told me off for being an idiot and pointed me in the right direction. In my defence there was a big blue arrow painted on the road so I just followed it like some primitive creature that knows nothing other than to follow the colour blue. I was also really tired.

The next few kilometres were about grinding through. I had 10km remaining as a milestone which, once I'd crossed it I'd feel like I was pretty much on the back stretch and could relax.

Getting to 10km remaining was easier said than done. Taking breaks more frequently, I still couldn't summon any energy for some of the longer climbs. This section was more hilly and further from the coast. I was also just less aware of my surroundings than previously, because I was too focussed on travelling in a straight line.

## 15km to go

Eventually I made it to the top of the hilly bit, and had a nice downhill section that ate up the distance I was labouring to cover. 15km quickly became 12, where I stopped for a very long break before the final bridge. The bridge to Imabari.

[Said bridge](https://maps.app.goo.gl/n3fVuWhHSdFPhavH8)

It's pretty mega, super long. Like miles long. miles plural. Very spectacular to ride along, being buffeted by the wind from oncoming lorries and buses. Also just the wind in general being that high up and exposed.

I again tried to go fast on this bridge, but I don't think I realised how long it was, and how long I'd have to go fast for. It was also a long enough bridge that it undulated, going up and down hill in different parts. So I couldn't just hammer out a consistent speed on 7th gear. When I got to the end, was exhausted. 

## 7km to go

The next part was fun though, lots of downhill for ages pedalling 0 amount.

The last part was riding to victory towards Imabari. My legs were completely worn out and my vision was getting a bit shaky. I think having to concentrate pretty hard to stay upright, and being aware of traffic etc, it was quite mentally draining as well as physically.

Got to the end at about 4:45pm, nice and early. 8 hours door to door.

I popped in to the Lawsons next to the Imabari cycle terminal. The guy at the counter saw me shuffling around and asked if I was ok. I said I was just tired from my bike ride. I saw him again the next day as I grabbed a drink, we recognised each other and he said I was looking a lot better. I felt a lot better.

## Final thoughts

Overall really enjoyed the trip. If I did it again, I'd do it in 2 or 3 days, and try and take it all in a bit better. I was a bit too concerned with just making it to the finish that I didn't stop enough to fully appreciate it. Especially the latter parts. I don't regret doing it in one day though, it was a really tough but fun challenge and I felt pretty proud of myself afterwards. I think you also learn more about yourself when you push closer to limits. Like if I did this over 2 days I probably would have just gone hell for leather both times to finish as quick as possible, and not be forced into learning how to pace myself. These learnings may be obvious to an experienced cyclist, who probably could have told me this before I set off, but there's something to be said for learning things by yourself through experience. That's my excuse anyway and I'm sticking with it.

The sights and scenery were amazing, probably the biggest highlight. The trip is very accessible for tourists or amateurs, and well maintatined with rest stops and road markings. Very easy to recommend to anyone who is potentially interested. They even hire out e-bikes for anyone who wants to cheat. Yes, e-bikes are cheating and they don't count. Just take the bus if you're that lazy. It's quicker and cheaper.
