Ok so first things first we're not technically in Ube, but close to it. In an onsen called Katakura, in Yamaguchi prefecture. You take the train to Tokonami station if that helps.

So leaving Hiroshima, had a bit of breakfast and checked out. Walked to the station, took a few trains to get to Hiroshima station, then booked the bullet train.

When I booked the accommodation I thought I'd gone for somewhere near Hiroshima station, which would have been a good idea because that's where everything was. But I didn't so it wasn't. It was actually a bit of a trek to be honest. Mum's foot was sore from too much walking and I felt a bit guilty about that. I have started to learn the value of this sort of convenience while on holiday. When things are close to the station it makes things so much easier. Maybe the lesson is just to be less of a cheapskate.

Anyway, bullet train tickets bought, we headed to the platform. The train was only 10 minutes away, whereas the last time we took Shinkansen we had to wait almost an hour.

Waiting for us on the platform was a lady who'd dropped her coat down between the train and the platform. Clearly didn't mind the gap.

A conductor came over and told her to wait for the next train. Our train.

When said train arrived, it stopped short of the dropped coat, and so short of our gate to enter from.

Mum took off down the platform to get back to the right carriage, paying no mind to the announcement saying the doors were closing. Also paying no mind to me *yelling* at her to get on the train. Those who know me know that I rarely yell, and this was a proper barking. Anyway she didn't hear me so I needn't have bothered.

A train conductor told me to get on the train, as I was standing in the doorway waving at mum's back to get on the train, exasperated. I did was the conductor told me, because I'm more scared about being told off by staff than I am of being losing my mum in a foreign country. I was pretty confident the conductor would have shepherded mum on board, but it wasn't until I saw her in the carriage ahead that I could relax. It wasn't until about halfway to Shin-Yamaguchi that I *did* relax.

In hindsight it was pretty funny, the mental image of mum running down the platform right next to the train she could have just gotten on. I guess in the heat of the moment it's hard to think straight. 

At Shin-Yamaguchi we changed to a local train. It was one of those cute 2-car ones, filled with locals. I could tell one was a local because she had a catapillar on her jacket sleeve. Like a real one just crawling around that she must have just not noticed.

We were picked up from the station by the owner of the ryōkan. I was glad because otherwise the journey may have been a bit fiddly.

The owner was very friendly and chatty, the sort of person everyone says to expect in Japan. He pointed out a few local landmarks, such as the supermarket and ramen-ya.

We were taken to our rooms, thankfully we could go in before our 3pm check-in, having arrived around 1.

Overall the journey, which I was pretty worried about, was very smooth. Weirdly the only thing that nearly went wrong was the train being "unreliable", which is more the girl who didn't mind the gap's fault than the train's.

After catching up on writing, we headed down to the onsen. Mum's first rodeo, not mine. We headed off to our respective baths, prepared with towels and soaps. Both of us got a bit of a chat with locals, neither of us understood much of what was being said. It's nice here because people don't really speak English aside from what they probably learned in school 40 years ago. It forced me to dust of my Japanese again, albeit in very broken sentences. I probably haven't done as well with the language as I would have liked, but when everyting is so new it's tough on the brain to also generate Japanese language sentences. I tried my best though.

We reconvened after an hour and a half or so, both feeling quite refreshed. The onsen is a bit smaller and less industrial feeling than the one in Hokkaido, which is neither good nor bad in my opinion, just different. Maybe I enjoyed this one more, as I'm just slightly more relaxed now that I'm settled in Japan. Maybe because this place is a little less fancy, which is more to my preference. Maybe it was nice just having a chat with a stranger.

After onsen we had a call with my brother. It's mother's day so it was good to catch up. Mum clearly wanted to stop talking to us and get dinner, so after we hung up we headed out to the local restaurant - for sushi! It was really good, I'll share a photo of it at some point. Popped into 7-11 for tomorrow's breakfast, then back home for the night. 

Our ryokan doesn't seem to have much in the way of eating and drinking spaces, unless it does and we just haven't found it. But never mind, I'm happy having stuff from the shops to a certain extent. Mum will definitely complain though if she doesn't get her tea. Will figure it out tomorrow
